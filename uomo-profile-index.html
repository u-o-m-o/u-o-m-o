Conclusions

  • Usercentric vision:
    From our analysis of social media clearly emerged the dominant position of users. Users make sense of the existence of social media.

  • Users make use of social media platforms in several ways:
    • As a showcase of themselves providing a certain amount of information in a sort of digital portfolio, where lists of contents are collected and organized by topics
    • As a communication tool that allows to keep in contact with friends and acquaintances, as well as to create new linkages with strangers starting worthwhile networking

  • Technical conclusions related to already existing ontologies

  • It is possible to easily create fake accounts, meaning that the user’s profile does not match a real person. Obviously, a physical real person always lies behind fake identities. The user hides himself behind an alias or alter-ego, customizing his profile with information that does not match his real personality.

Who is Demetra Hanna?

In order to have a consistent overview of how SM personal profiles are structured and how they work, we created a fake identity called Demetra Hanna Davis Keys.

Her name was inspired by our course anagram - DHDK. On behalf of Demetra Hanna, we subscribed to 18 social media and analyzed each profile.

Creating a fake identity enabled us to have a deeper understanding of social media that we didn’t know before this research.
This was an important part of our case study, given that we wanted to provide a broad picture on the existing social media.

As a consequence, thanks to this procedure we, unintentionally, proved the fact that social media accept fake identities and that users are actually free to present themselves as they wish.

Case Study

We selected 18 different social medias. For each of them we analyzed the structure of the users’ profile. We picked the most famous social medias like Facebook, Instagram, Twitter and LinkedIn, but also very peculiar ones whose features are related to specific fields, such as music, books, video games and networks. We came up with an interesting and varied list of elements/objects and we compared the results in a spreadsheet.

Afterwhile, for each SM we reported whether those features are supported or not. After creating an algorithm, we were able to determine which was the % of each element in comparison to the entire sample of Social Media we chose. Results are explained in Preliminary Stats section.

As we expected, some of the features we highlighted are present in almost all social medias (eg. nickname, real name, profile picture, some personal description or bio, followers, following).

Other features, such as posts and lists of contents, are present almost in every Social Media, but they are very differentiated (semantically equivalent objects). So, for example, while in Facebook, Instagram, MySpace, Twitter the lists of content are mainly galleries of pictures and videos, in SoundCloud and YouTube they are playlists (songs or videos). Whereas, the majority of the features are very specific and, as a consequence, they are related just to few SMs or even just to a single one (unique profile features).

The results are summarized in 3 charts that you will find in the Preliminary stats section.

State of Art

    Among the already existing ontologies, the most useful ones for UOMO ontology were SIOC Ontology and FOAF ontology.
  • SIOC provides the main concepts and properties required to describe information from online communities (e.g., message boards, wikis, weblogs, etc.) on the Semantic Web (Berrueta, 2010).
  • The purpose of FOAF Ontology is to allow people define their profile, content and relationship to other people and contents so as to interconnect all web resources in a distributed manner.
